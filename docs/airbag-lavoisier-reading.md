# How Lavoisier Weighed the Invisible: The Birth of Conservation of Mass

## Reading

In 1774, almost three hundred years ago, a Parisian tax collector named Antoine-Laurent Lavoisier walked into his private laboratory with a problem that had haunted chemistry for a century. When things burned, they seemed to *lose* mass — a log turned to a little pile of ash that weighed almost nothing. But when metals were heated, they seemed to *gain* mass — the dull gray surface of tin turned into a heavier, crumbly white powder. Every chemist of the day thought something was flowing into or out of the burning substance: a mysterious fluid they called **phlogiston**. No one had ever seen phlogiston. No one could weigh it. But for a hundred years, the theory had survived anyway, because nobody had designed an experiment clean enough to kill it.

Lavoisier was going to kill it. And he was going to do it with a balance.

### Why a Balance Was a Weapon

Lavoisier did not have fancy instruments by today's standards. He had no thermometers more precise than a few tenths of a degree, no gas sensors, no microscopes that could see atoms. What he *did* have — because he had married into money and funded his own lab — was a balance so precise it could detect a change of a fraction of a grain, about the mass of a single grain of salt. In an era when most chemists worked by smell, color, and rough weight, Lavoisier weighed everything. He weighed the glassware. He weighed the air above the reaction. He weighed the ash, the residue, the water vapor he trapped in cold tubes. His whole laboratory was built around the obsessive premise that **if mass changed, something had entered or left — and he was going to catch it.**

His wife, Marie-Anne Paulze-Lavoisier, was a full scientific partner: she learned English specifically so she could translate the papers of British chemists like Joseph Priestley and Henry Cavendish for her husband, and she drew the precise illustrations of the apparatus we still see in textbooks today. Without her, the experiments below would not have been connected to what the rest of Europe was doing.

### Experiment 1: The Sealed Tin (1774)

The English chemist Robert Boyle had done a famous experiment almost a century earlier. Boyle put a small amount of tin inside a glass retort (a sealed vessel with a long, curved neck), sealed it, and heated it. When the tin had converted to a crumbly white calx — what we now call tin oxide — Boyle broke the seal, weighed the calx, and found it heavier than the tin he started with. Boyle concluded that "fire particles" had passed *through* the walls of the glass and added mass to the tin.

Lavoisier repeated the experiment. But he did one thing Boyle had not. He weighed the **entire sealed apparatus — retort, tin, and the air inside** — before heating. He heated it until the tin had fully converted to calx. Then, before opening anything, he weighed the entire apparatus *again*.

The mass was **unchanged**.

Only *then* did he crack the seal. He heard a hiss as outside air rushed *in*. He weighed the opened retort. The mass had now *increased*, by exactly the mass of the air that had rushed in. He separated the calx from the remaining tin and weighed it — the tin had gained weight, by exactly the amount of air consumed from inside the sealed vessel.

Nothing had passed through the glass. Nothing had been created or destroyed. The tin had combined with a portion of the air that was *already trapped inside the retort with it*. Boyle had been wrong for a century because he had broken the seal before weighing.

### Experiment 2: Twelve Days with Mercury (1774–1775)

The tin experiment proved something had come out of the air — but Lavoisier did not yet know *what*. To find out, he set up the most famous piece of glassware in the history of chemistry: a retort partially filled with liquid mercury, its long neck curving up and into an inverted bell jar standing in a trough of mercury. The arrangement sealed a known volume of ordinary air above the mercury pool.

Then he lit a fire under the retort — and kept it burning for **twelve days**.

Over those twelve days, the surface of the mercury slowly developed red specks, then patches, then a crust of red calx (what we now call mercuric oxide, HgO). As it did, the level of mercury in the bell jar slowly rose, because the volume of air above it was shrinking. When the reaction finished after nearly two weeks, almost exactly **one-fifth of the original air had disappeared**. Lavoisier tested the air that was left: he lowered a lit candle into it, and the flame died. He placed a mouse in it, and the mouse could not breathe. He named this remaining gas **azote** — Greek for "no life." We call it nitrogen.

Then he scraped the red calx off the mercury, collected it, and heated it more intensely in a separate apparatus. A gas came off. This gas made a glowing splint burst into flame. It kept a mouse alive longer than normal air. And the volume of gas released was, within his margin of measurement, **exactly equal** to the volume of air that had been consumed during the twelve days. Lavoisier named this gas **oxygène**.

The missing one-fifth of the air was now locked inside the red calx. When he released it, it came back out. Mass was conserved on a scale no one had ever measured before: across 12 days, two glass vessels, a fire, and a reaction that produced an invisible gas, every atom was accounted for.

### What Had To Be True for Any of This to Work

None of Lavoisier's conclusions were inevitable. They required choices in experimental design that seem obvious now and were revolutionary then:

- **The apparatus had to be sealed.** An open vessel would have let gases in or out undetected, and conservation of mass would never have been visible.
- **The total system — not just the "substance" — had to be weighed.** If he had weighed only the tin, he would have seen an apparent mass increase and invented a new phlogiston to explain it. Weighing the retort *and* its contents revealed that the total was unchanged.
- **The precision had to exceed the effect.** A change of 1/5 of the air in a small retort is a few hundredths of a gram. A butcher's scale would have missed it.
- **The time had to be long enough for the reaction to finish.** Twelve days of continuous heating is not impatience. Stopping at day three would have given a partial conversion and an incorrect mass balance.
- **The products had to be identified, not just weighed.** Testing the residual gas with a candle and a mouse was the difference between "something disappeared" and "oxygen was consumed."
- **The assumptions had to be dropped.** Every chemist before Lavoisier believed air was a simple, single substance. Accepting that air was a *mixture* — part of which could react and part of which could not — required abandoning a belief older than chemistry itself.

Twenty years later, Lavoisier was accused of embezzlement by the French revolutionary government and sent to the guillotine. The judge who sentenced him reportedly said, "The Republic has no need of scientists." The mathematician Joseph-Louis Lagrange wrote the day after: "It took them only a moment to cut off that head, and a hundred years will not be enough to produce another like it."

But Lavoisier left behind something the Republic could not execute: a method. Weigh everything. Seal the system. Exceed the precision of the effect. Name the products. Drop the prior belief. That method is why an airbag can inflate to exactly the right volume in thirty milliseconds — and why, in this unit, you can calculate how many grams of sodium azide must be sealed inside the steering wheel before anyone ever pulls the trigger.
